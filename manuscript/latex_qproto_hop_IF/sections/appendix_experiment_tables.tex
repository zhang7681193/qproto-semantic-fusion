The first two tables isolate equal-byte rows from the UCI HAR and UCI Multiple Features main-text tables. The MHEALTH table gives the full wearable multi-source result summarized in the main text. PAMAP2 is included as a larger wearable boundary case where adaptive CHOP falls back to low-order CProto. Hydraulic Systems adds a real industrial condition-monitoring check: compact CProto/CHOP statistics are far above shared-view compression and close to PCA/ridge/late-fusion baselines, while HeMIS and mask-aware MLP remain stronger full-dimensional trainable references. WDBC is included as a biomedical breadth check. It is nearly saturated, so it is less diagnostic than UCI HAR, Multiple Features, MHEALTH, or Hydraulic Systems; the main text therefore treats it as a boundary case rather than a headline comparison. Gas Drift is reported as a negative boundary check: random and temporal splits both show that CHOP is not naturally favored there, even after cross-sensor HOP pair selection and a small HOP-weight screen.

\paragraph{Stability and claim boundaries.}
The seed-level tables should be read with the scoped claims in Table~\ref{tab:claim-evidence-map}. The most stable comparisons are the schema-correct rows against no-schema, wrong-schema, shared-view, and equal-byte RFF controls. UCI HAR, MHEALTH, and Multiple Features also show stable advantages over equal-byte shared-view compression. Some comparisons are intentionally not used as headline claims: MFeat random-key CProto is strong, so that benchmark supports schema-aware low-order fusion rather than selected-key superiority; adaptive CHOP on MHEALTH has the highest mean among compact equal-byte statistics but is reported as interaction-heavy evidence rather than as a universal HOP guarantee; and Qiskit Aer rows are compatibility checks with limited matched seeds, not hardware validation.
